# Style Guide: Avoiding AI-Like Prose

## Editorial Sources

This guide combines article-derived writing advice with modern editorial guidance from Nature and Elsevier. Treat the rules below as derived recommendations, not absolute laws.

Key source themes:

- Nature: use clear language, avoid unnecessary jargon, avoid `obviously` and `clearly`, and keep titles and abstracts accessible.
- Nature: avoid puns and questions in titles when a direct title will do.
- Elsevier: use active voice when possible, keep prose clear and concise, and check tense and grammar carefully.

## What Makes Prose Sound AI-Like

AI-like prose usually has one or more of these traits:

- vague framing
- generic filler
- inflated claims
- repetitive sentence openings
- over-hedging
- abstract nouns without concrete detail
- self-conscious commentary about the writing itself

## Fix-It Rules

### 1. Replace abstraction with a concrete subject and action

Bad:

- `This study explores the complex relationship between...`

Better:

- `We examined the association between ...`

Bad:

- `There is a tendency for ...`

Better:

- `In this cohort, ...`

### 2. Remove filler openers

Delete or rewrite unless they add a real fact or transition:

- `It is worth noting that ...`
- `In the current era ...`
- `It should be emphasized that ...`
- `Needless to say ...`

### 3. Do not use vague conclusory phrases when a specific claim is available

Bad:

- `The findings highlight the importance of the intervention.`

Better:

- `The intervention reduced X by Y% in Z patients.`

### 4. Use precise hedging, not weak hedging

Bad:

- `The results may possibly suggest that ...`

Better:

- `The results suggest that ...`

### 5. Prefer verbs over nominalizations

Bad:

- `We made an assessment of the association ...`

Better:

- `We assessed the association ...`

Bad:

- `There was a reduction in ...`

Better:

- `... decreased ...`

### 6. Replace weak intensifiers and empty emphasis

Avoid unless truly needed:

- `very`
- `really`
- `clearly`
- `obviously`
- `basically`
- `significantly` when the statistic is not actually significant

If the point matters, show the data instead of intensifying the sentence.

### 7. Keep one sentence to one job

If a sentence tries to do all of the following, split it:

- state the result
- explain the result
- justify the result
- compare the result with prior work

### 8. Vary sentence openings

Repeated openings like these feel machine-generated:

- `This study...`
- `The results...`
- `It is...`
- `There was...`

Vary by starting with:

- the actor
- the result
- the setting
- the comparison

### 9. Make titles searchable and specific

Prefer:

- short
- direct
- descriptive
- free of jargon when possible

Avoid:

- puns
- questions
- inflated metaphors
- vague nouns such as `study`, `analysis`, `investigation` when the content can be named directly

## Rewrite Patterns

- `This study explores...` -> `We studied...`
- `It is worth noting that...` -> delete unless the sentence adds a specific implication
- `In the current era...` -> delete
- `It is obvious from Figure 2 that...` -> `Figure 2 shows that...`
- `More research is needed...` -> `A prospective study should test ...`
- `The results highlight the importance of...` -> `The results show that ...`
- `There is evidence that...` -> `In this cohort, ...`

## Final Pass Checklist

Before finalizing, check:

- Is every sentence carrying data, logic, or necessary context?
- Did I remove filler that exists only to sound academic?
- Did I keep the tone confident but not inflated?
- Did I avoid overusing jargon, acronyms, and abstraction?
- Did I avoid repeating the same idea in multiple sentences?
- Would a clinician or journal editor understand the point on one read?
