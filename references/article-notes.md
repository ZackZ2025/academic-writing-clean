## 1) Planning and Outline

From `PMC3178846`, use a two-level outline before writing.

Level 1 questions:

- What is the topic?
- Why is it important?
- What is the hypothesis?
- What are the results?
- What is the major finding?

Level 2 sections:

- Introduction
- Methods
- Results
- Discussion and Conclusions

Practical rule:

- Build the outline from your data, not from the order in which the experiments happened.
- Update the outline again after Methods and Results are drafted.
- Discuss the outline with a mentor or colleague before polishing sentences.

## 2) Title and Abstract

`PMC6904852` recommends generating multiple titles, then choosing the clearest one after outside feedback.

Useful title pattern:

- `X is associated with greater Y in Z, a multicenter cohort study`

Why it works:

- It is specific.
- It signals the study design.
- It avoids metaphor and vagueness.

From `PMC4473859`, keep the title simple and informative.

- Good title qualities: short, accurate, specific, searchable.
- Avoid: abbreviations, jargon, formulas, and low-value openers such as `Study of`, `Investigation into`, and `Observations on`.
- Report the subject of the paper, not the conclusion.

Abstract rules from `PMC4434439` and `PMC4473859`:

- Make the abstract definitive, not descriptive.
- Make it stand alone.
- Keep it factual.
- Do not add undefined abbreviations.
- Do not cite references in the abstract.
- Do not waste words on generic scene-setting.

## 3) Methods

From `PMC3178846`, write Methods so another researcher can repeat the work.

Bad:

- `Bacteria were pelleted by centrifugation.`

Better:

- `Bacteria were pelleted by centrifugation at the specified speed, time, and temperature.`

Bad:

- `To isolate T cells, lymph nodes were collected.`

Better:

- `To isolate T cells, specify the source tissue, animal strain, timing, and collection conditions.`

Practical rules:

- State the sample, setting, inclusion criteria, exclusion criteria, procedure, and statistical approach.
- Use one consistent point of view within a paragraph.
- Do not switch back and forth between passive and active voice for no reason.
- If a method is already established, cite it instead of over-explaining it.
- Include ethics approval, consent, and conflict-of-interest information when relevant.

## 4) Results

From `PMC3178846` and `PMC4473859`, Results should be factual and selective.

Bad:

- `Table 3 clearly shows that ...`
- `It is obvious from Figure 4 that ...`

Better:

- `Table 3 shows that ...`
- `Figure 4 shows that ...`

Bad:

- `We tested the hypothesis that there is a disruption of membrane asymmetry.`

Better:

- `We tested whether membrane asymmetry is disrupted.`

Bad:

- `In this paper we provide an argument that stem cells repopulate injured organs.`

Better:

- `In this paper we argue that stem cells repopulate injured organs.`

Practical rules:

- Present representative data, not every raw value.
- Keep negative findings when they affect interpretation.
- Do not repeat the same result in text, table, and figure unless that repetition truly helps the reader.
- Do not smuggle interpretation into the Results unless the journal style allows a combined Results/Discussion format.

## 5) Introduction

`PMC3178846` uses a three-move logic:

- establish the territory
- find the niche
- occupy the niche

Practical version:

- Start with the broad clinical or scientific context.
- Narrow to the specific gap or limitation in prior work.
- End by stating exactly what this paper does and why it matters.

Avoid the "suspense" approach:

- Do not hide the question or the main finding until later sections.
- Do not make the reader work to discover the study aim.
- Keep the Introduction concise and purposeful.

## 6) Discussion

From `PMC3178846`, the Discussion should open with the main finding.

Useful openers:

- `Our findings demonstrate ...`
- `In this study, we have shown that ...`
- `Our results suggest ...`

Three-move structure:

- Major finding, its meaning, and alternative explanations
- Research context, discrepancies, limitations
- Closing take-home message, implications, or practical advice

Bad ending:

- `More research is needed.`

Better ending:

- `A prospective study in X population should test whether Y changes Z.`

Practical rules:

- Explain why the result matters.
- Compare your findings with the literature.
- Address unexpected findings directly.
- State limitations without letting them erase the contribution.
- End with a specific, defensible message.

## 7) Revision and Submission

From `PMC6904852` and `PMC4434439`:

- Draft early, revise often, and separate macroediting from line editing.
- Macroediting: logic, structure, section order, missing content.
- Microediting: sentence flow, word choice, tense, punctuation, grammar.
- Read the paper aloud or print it out to catch awkward prose.
- Ask colleagues to review it before submission.
- Do not assume the first draft is final.

Submission checklist:

- Is the paper relevant to the journal?
- Is it styled for the journal?
- Are the conclusions justified by the results?
- Are the tables and figures clear and non-redundant?
- Are limitations and conflicts of interest stated?

## 8) Fast Reference Patterns

- Outline first, then draft.
- Methods and Results first when the paper feels blocked.
- Introduction after the contribution is clear.
- Discussion from main finding to context to limitations.
- Title and Abstract last.

## 9) Negative Examples Worth Reusing

- `Study of ...`
- `Observations on ...`
- `Investigation into ...`
- `It is obvious that ...`
- `Clearly, ...`
- `More research is needed.`
- `This study explores ...` when a direct verb would be better
